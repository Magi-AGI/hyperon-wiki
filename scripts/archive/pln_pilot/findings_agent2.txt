## §1. Truth-Value Semantics

**C-1-1:** PLN truth-values are multi-component objects; the simplest type is the *SimpleTruthValue* consisting of a pair `<s,w>` where `s ∈ [0,1]` is a *strength* (a probability) and `w ∈ [0,1]` is a *confidence* or *weight of evidence* giving qualitative credence to the strength estimate. `<.6,1>` means probability .6 known with absolute certainty; `<.6,0>` means strength value is meaningless and equivalent to `<x,0>` for any x. [Source: Ch. 1 §1.4 ~p. 14; Ch. 2 §2.1 ~p. 23]

**C-1-2:** The `<s,w>` form has a dual representation `<s,N>` where `N` is a *count* (positive number giving total amount of evidence), related by the heuristic `w = N/(N+k)` for an adjustable parameter `k`. `<s,w>` is the default; count-form is explicitly flagged when used. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.1 ~p. 23–24]

**C-1-3:** The primary PLN truth-value in current practice is the *IndefiniteTruthValue*, a quadruple `<[L,U], b, k>` where `[L,U]` is a probability interval, `b ∈ [0,1]` is a *credibility level*, and `k` is an integer *lookahead* parameter. Semantics: "I assign probability `b` to the hypothesis that after `k` more observations the ordinary (first-order) probability of S will lie in `[L,U]`." [Source: Ch. 2 §2.1 ~p. 24; Ch. 4 §4.2 ~p. 50; Ch. 4 §4.4 ~p. 54]

**C-1-4:** When `k` is held constant system-wide, indefinite TVs are written as `<[L,U], b>` triples, and `k` "essentially cancels out" during inference so long as it is the same in premises and conclusion. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.1 ~p. 24; Ch. 4 §4.4 ~p. 54]

**C-1-5:** If `b = 1` the indefinite-TV approach reduces to Walley-style imprecise probabilities; `b < 1` is what lets inferential intervals remain narrower than the degenerate `[0,1]` endpoints typical of imprecise-probability chaining. [Source: Ch. 4 §4.2 ~p. 51]

**C-1-6:** *DistributionalTruthValues* are discretized approximations to full probability distributions. With DistributionalTVs, PLN deduction reduces to matrix multiplication and inversion reduces to matrix inversion. Two sub-flavors exist: StepFunctionTruthValues and PolynomialTruthValues. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.1 ~p. 24 fn. 1]

**C-1-7:** Granularity hierarchy of TV approximations to a full distribution: single number < interval probability < indefinite quadruple < distributional TV (matrix). [Source: Ch. 1 §1.4 ~p. 14]

**C-1-8:** An IndefiniteTruthValue object is *in practice* endowed with three additional parameters beyond `<[L,U], b, k>`: (a) a symmetric-vs-asymmetric interval-type flag; (b) a family of second-order distributions; (c) a family of first-order distributions. So a TV object implicitly encodes one complex second-order distribution. [Source: Ch. 4 §4.4 ~p. 58]

**C-1-9:** Walley's imprecise probability interval has form `[m/(n+k), (m+k)/(n+k)]` where `n` is total observations, `m` is positive observations, and `k` the lookahead/learning parameter; indefinite probabilities generalize this. [Source: Ch. 4 §4.2 ~p. 50]

**C-1-10:** Indefinite probabilities are internally justified via a three-level probability hierarchy for direct evaluation: (i) second-order distribution whose means are drawn from `[L_1, U_1] ⊇ [L,U]`; (ii) first-order distributions with means drawn from the second-order distribution. Monte Carlo integration yields the inferential `[L_f, U_f]`. [Source: Ch. 6 §6.1 ~p. 131–133]

**C-1-11:** Quantified statements (ForAll / ThereExists / fuzzy) receive *third-order* probabilities: the second-order level represents the mean for `F(x)`; the third-order level "perturbs" the second-order distribution by varying `x`. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.1 ~p. 240; §11.2 ~p. 240–242]

**C-1-12:** The conversion between `<[L,U], b, k>` and `<s,n>` uses the theorem: if `x` successes were seen in `n` Bernoulli trials with uniform prior, the probability of `X` further successes in the next `k` trials is `(n+1) * C(k,X) * C(n,x) / [(k+n+1) * C(k+n, X+x)]`. This is used to solve inversely for `n` given `L,U,b,k`. [Source: Ch. 4 §4.5.1 ~p. 59–60]

**C-1-13:** A fast inverse-function approximation fits the form `n = -ln(b/A) / B` with `A,B` bilinear functions of `L,U` and (optionally) `k`. Numerical coefficients `A_{ijk}`, `B_{ijk}` are given explicitly (e.g., `A_{111} = -0.00875486`, `A_{122} = -0.220372781`, etc.). [Source: Ch. 4 §4.5.1.2 ~p. 61–62]

**C-1-14:** *First-order* PLN TVs apply to relationships between simple terms; *higher-order* TVs apply to predicates / relationships between relationships. The truth-value formulas are the *same* between first-order and corresponding higher-order rules (Implication ↔ Inheritance, Equivalence ↔ Similarity), so HOI largely reuses FOI formulas. [Source: Ch. 1 §1.3 ~p. 12–13; Ch. 2 §2.4 ~p. 27–28; Ch. 10 §10.3 ~p. 205–206]

**C-1-15:** *Extensional* vs *intensional* distinction is treated asymmetrically in PLN (unlike NARS which treats them symmetrically). Extensional is primary: `Subset A B <s>` means `s = P(x ∈ B | x ∈ A)`. Intensional is *derived*: `IntensionalInheritance A B` is defined as `Subset A_PAT B_PAT` where `A_PAT` is the pattern-set of A. [Source: Ch. 1 §1.3 ~p. 11–12; Ch. 2 §2.4.1.2 ~p. 30; Ch. 12 §12.3 ~p. 255]

**C-1-16:** Composite `Inheritance A B <tv>` is defined as `OR(Subset A B, IntensionalInheritance A B)`. Using both ASSOC and PAT versions of intensional inheritance, one has three-way disjunction: `Inheritance_ASSOC_PAT = OR(Subset, IntInh_ASSOC, IntInh_PAT)`. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.4.1.2 ~p. 30; Ch. 12 §12.3 ~p. 256]

**C-1-17:** The `Member A B <t>` relationship represents *fuzzy set membership* (not probabilistic). Member TVs combine via min/max (or product/max) algebra; `t = 0.5` denotes partial membership, not uncertainty. Member is the *only* place fuzzy semantics lives in PLN. [Source: Ch. 1 §1.6.1 ~p. 17–18; Ch. 2 §2.3 ~p. 26–27]

**C-1-18:** Subset TV for fuzzy-member-based definition: `s = Σ_x f(Member(x,B).s, Member(x,A).s) / Σ_x Member(x,A).s` where `f(x,y)` is the fuzzy intersection — current practice uses `f = min`. Alternative is `f(x,y) = x*y`. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.4.1.1 ~p. 28–29]

**C-1-19:** PLN distinguishes at least four semantics for "Jim is tall .6": MemberLink (partial fuzzy membership), IntensionalInheritance/Subset (shares .6 of tall-properties), composite Inheritance (weighted avg of ext+int), and the Walley-style "after-more-info" interpretation. All four are distinguishable in PLN but blurred in common fuzzy-logic usage. [Source: Ch. 1 §1.6.1 ~p. 18]

**C-1-20:** PLN TVs are defined *relative to a Context*. The default context is the entire universe (usually not useful). `Context C R A B <t>` is defined by the equivalence `Context C R A B <t> ≡ R (A AND C) (B AND C) <t>`. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.2 ~p. 25–26; Ch. 10 §10.11.3 ~p. 233]

**C-1-21:** Context is the "implicit universe" for probability calculations; `|U|` (context size) affects inference results, especially deduction. Smaller `U` ⇒ more speculative inferences; larger `U` ⇒ less error but less creativity. There is no additional computational cost to a larger `|U|`. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.5 ~p. 32–34; Ch. 5 §5.2.1 ~p. 68]

**C-1-22:** The *Optimal Universe Size* formula: given known triple-intersection count `n_ABC` and binary intersection counts, `n_U = n_B + (n_A - n_AB)(n_C - n_BC) / (n_AC - n_ABC)`. Averaging across many known triples in a context yields a good `n_U` estimate. Tested on a newspaper corpus: formula predicted `|U| ≈ 650,000` within 20,000 from a few hundred triples. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.3 ~p. 87–89]

**C-1-23:** Term truth-value: `A <tv>` ≡ `Subset Universe A <tv>`. So a term's strength is the fraction of the (possibly implicit) universe falling into category A. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.5 ~p. 32]

**C-1-24:** Terms have *multiple* term-probabilities in the full intensional framework: an extensional term probability, and intensional term probabilities (ASSOC-based and PAT-based). Composite `P_Inheritance(X) = OR(P(X), P_ASS(X), P_PAT(X))`. [Source: Ch. 12 §12.3.1 ~p. 257–258]

**C-1-25:** Revision rule for simple TVs (heuristic default): given `<s_1, n_1>` and `<s_2, n_2>`, form `w_1 = n_1/(n_1+n_2)`, `w_2 = n_2/(n_1+n_2)`, and `s = w_1 s_1 + w_2 s_2` with `n = n_1 + n_2 - c·min(n_1, n_2)` where `c ∈ [0,1]` reflects assumed redundancy (c=1 completely redundant, c=0 distinct). [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10.2 ~p. 116]

**C-1-26:** Sharper revision rule when both premises came from sub-samples: `s_3 = (x_1 + x_2 - x_1 x_2/u) / (y_1 + y_2 - y_1 y_2/u)` where `x_i = |A ∩ B ∩ C_i|`, `y_i = |A ∩ C_i|`, `u = |U|`. Simplifies, assuming `y_1 = y_2 = q`, to `s_3 = (s_1 + s_2 - s_1 s_2 c) / (2 - c)` where `c = q/u`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10.3–§5.10.4 ~p. 117–120]

**C-1-27:** The averaging revision formula `s_3 = (s_1 + s_2)/2` corresponds to assuming an *infinite* universe (c→0). With finite `c`, revision iteratively drifts strengths upward; `c` must be dynamically tuned per domain to avoid drift-to-1. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10.4 ~p. 120–121]

**C-1-28:** Revision with weight of evidence: introduce `v_1 = 2 d_1 / (d_1 + d_2)`, `v_2 = 2 - v_1`, yielding `s_3 = (v_1 x_1 + v_2 x_2 - x_1 x_2 / u) / (v_1 y_1 + v_2 y_2 - y_1 y_2 / u)` and corresponding heuristic `s_3 = (v_1 s_1 + v_2 s_2 - s_1 s_2 c) / (2 - c)`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10.5 ~p. 121–122]

**C-1-29:** TV arithmetic: with extended count range `[-∞,∞]` allowing *negative* evidence (to accommodate proven-fraudulent observations), one can define `T + S = W(W^{-1}T + W^{-1}S)` and scalar multiplication `cT = W(W^{-1}c · W^{-1}T)`. Under this, TV addition *is* the PLN revision rule (weighted average in strength, sum in count); scalar multiplication preserves strength and scales count. The operations form a 2-D vector space over TVs. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10.6 ~p. 122–125]

**C-1-30:** The mapping `W` is `W(x,y) = ((1 + x/y)/2, y)` with inverse `W^{-1}(x,y) = (y(2x-1), y)`. This is the unique "natural" nonlinear map making TV addition equal PLN revision. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10.6 ~p. 124]

**C-1-31:** Revision with indefinite TVs: take second-order distributions `D_1, D_2` with counts `n_1, n_2`, form `D = w_1 D_1 + w_2 D_2` (w's from counts), then re-extract `<[L,U], b, k>` as usual. Example: revising `<[0.1, 0.2], 0.9, 20>` and `<[0.3, 0.7], 0.9, 20>` (counts 56.6558 and 6.48493) yields `<[0.13614, 0.23321], 0.9, 20>` with count 52.67. [Source: Ch. 6 §6.2.5 ~p. 139–140]

**C-1-32:** Two *premise-consistency constraints* are enforced in FOI TV formulas via Heaviside functions on `sAB`: `max((sA+sB-1)/sA, 0) ≤ sAB ≤ min(1, sB/sA)`, and similarly for `sBC`. Formulas evaluate to zero outside these regions. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.2.2.2 ~p. 74; Ch. 5 §5.4 ~p. 90–93]

**C-1-33:** *Rule of Choice*: when revision is inappropriate (conflicting evidence with high weight, or circular inference trail violations), PLN may instead (a) create a distributional TV if the conflict is due to disjoint evidence sets, (b) keep versions as Hypothetical pass-through atoms, (c) inferentially merge trails — expensive, (d) fall back to revision with `d_conclusion = max(d_1, d_2)` instead of sum, as the default cheap alternative. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.12 ~p. 127–129]

**C-1-34:** Second-order distributional interpretation of the deduction rule (proof-theoretic, not betting): average `P(C|A)` over all triples `(A,B,C)` drawn from a universe `U` satisfying the given absolute/conditional probability constraints — the PLN formula is the *mean* of this second-order distribution, rather than the result of an ad-hoc independence assumption. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.2.2.2 ~p. 74–79 — Theorem 1]

## §2. Rule Taxonomy

### Family A — First-order term-logic core (Ch. 5)

**C-2-1:** **Inversion (≈ Bayes rule for PLN).** Given `sA, sB, sAB`, derive `sBA = sAB sA / sB`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.4 ~p. 89–90]

**C-2-2:** **Independence-Assumption-Based Deduction.** `ExtInh A B <sAB>, ExtInh B C <sBC>` with node strengths `sA, sB, sC` → `ExtInh A C <sAC>` where `sAC = sAB·sBC + (1 - sAB)·(sC - sB·sBC)/(1 - sB)`. Derived heuristically via a bag-of-balls hypergeometric model; rigorously via averaging over universes. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.2.2 ~p. 69–74]

**C-2-3:** **Concept-Geometry-Based Deduction.** Replaces independence with assumption that sets are "cohesive" with geometric shape (e.g., intervals on a circle, k-spheres on (k+1)-sphere). Formula: `P(C|A) = P(B|A)·P(C|B)·[1 + G(A,B,C)·max(0, -1 + 1/P(A|B) + P(C|B))] + ¬B terms`. In 1-D form: `sAC = sAB·sBC / min(sAB + sBC, 1)` is the "f(x,y)" kernel. Counters the underestimation bias the independence rule creates for cohesive sets. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.6 ~p. 101–109]

**C-2-4:** **Induction.** `Subset B A <sBA>, Subset B C <sBC>` (+ `sA, sB, sC`) → `Subset A C <sAC>`. Formula: `sAC = (sBA·sBC·sB/sA) + (1 - sBA·sB/sA)·(sC - sB·sBC)/(1 - sB)`. Derived as inversion(premise 1) → deduction. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.4 ~p. 90]

**C-2-5:** **Abduction.** `Subset A B <sAB>, Subset C B <sCB>` (+ `sA, sB, sC`) → `Subset A C <sAC>`. Formula: `sAC = (sAB·sCB·sC/sB) + sC·(1 - sAB)·(1 - sCB)/(1 - sB)`. Derived as deduction composed with inversion applied to premise 2. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.4 ~p. 92]

**C-2-6:** **Transitive Similarity.** Given `simAB, simBC, sA, sB, sC`:
`simAC = 1 / (1/deduction(T1,T2) + 1/deduction(T3,T4) - 1)`
where `T1 = (1 + sB/sA)·simAB/(1 + simAB)`, `T2 = (1 + sC/sB)·simBC/(1 + simBC)`, and `T3, T4` are symmetric swaps. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.5 ~p. 93–94]

**C-2-7:** **sim2inh.** Given `simAB, sA, sB`: `sAB = (1 + sB/sA)·simAB/(1 + simAB)`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.5 ~p. 95]

**C-2-8:** **inh2sim.** Given `sAC, sCA`: `simAC = 1/(1/sAC + 1/sCA - 1)`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.5 ~p. 95]

**C-2-9:** **inh2sim (3-arg).** Given `sAC, sA, sC`: `simAC = 1 / [(1 + sA/sC)/sAC - 1]`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.5 ~p. 95]

**C-2-10:** **Deduction with known dependencies.** When the triple intersection `P(A∧B∧C)` or `P(A∧C∧¬B)` is known, skip the independence assumption and substitute directly — e.g., given `(A AND B AND C) <0.6>` the rule becomes `0.6·P(B)/P(A) + (1 - P(B|A))·[P(C) - P(B)·P(C|B)]/(1 - P(B))`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.2.2.3 ~p. 86–87]

**C-2-11:** **Modus Ponens.** `Inh A B <sAB>, A <sA> ⊢ B <sB>`. Formula: `sB = sAB·sA + c·(1 - sA)` with `c` a default-term-probability parameter (~0.02). Highly error-prone heuristic — used only when term-logic deduction cannot be applied. Conceptually different from NARS MP which uses a universal statement K. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.7.1 ~p. 110–111]

**C-2-12:** **Symmetric Modus Ponens (Similarity MP).** `Similarity A B <simAB>, A <sA> ⊢ B <sB>`. Formula: `sB = sA·simAB + c·(1 - sA)·(1 + simAB)`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.7.2 ~p. 111–112]

**C-2-13:** **Member-to-Inheritance (M2I).** `Member x A <tv1> ⊢ Inheritance {x} A <tv2>` where `tv2.strength = tv1.strength` and `tv2.confidence` is a discounted version (widened interval for indefinite TVs). Approximately correct only for cohesive A. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.8 ~p. 112–113]

**C-2-14:** **Inheritance-to-Member (I2M).** Inverse direction of M2I; keeps strength constant, decreases confidence analogously. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.8 ~p. 113]

**C-2-15:** **Term Probability Inference.** From `A <sA>, Inh A B <sAB> ⊢ B <sB>` with `sB = sA·sAB/sBA`. Just Bayes' rule in inverted form. Must participate in inference trails. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.9 ~p. 113–114]

**C-2-16:** **Revision (core rule).** `Subset A B <s_1, n_1>, Subset A B <s_2, n_2> ⊢ Subset A B <s, n>` with `s = (n_1 s_1 + n_2 s_2)/(n_1 + n_2)` and `n = n_1 + n_2 - c·min(n_1, n_2)`. Extended formulas handle universe size, weight of evidence, and indefinite TVs. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10 ~p. 116]

### Family B — Higher-Order & Variable Rules (Ch. 10)

**C-2-17:** **FOI-parallel HOI deduction.** `Implication A B, Implication B C ⊢ Implication A C` where A, B, C are predicates or variable-bearing expressions. Same strength formula as first-order deduction. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.3 ~p. 205–206]

**C-2-18:** **Evaluation + Implication combination.** `Evaluation isA Ben <tv1>, Implication isA isB <tv2> ⊢ Evaluation isB Ben <tv3>`. Strength formula: deduction of tv1,tv2 via chain of M2I → deduction → I2M. Confidence discounted by product `M2ICountMultiplier × I2MCountMultiplier` (or interval-width multipliers for indefinite TVs). [Source: Ch. 10 §10.5 ~p. 207–208]

**C-2-19:** **SatisfyingSet operator.** `S = SatisfyingSet P` is defined by `(Member X S).tv = (Evaluation P X).tv`. Provides the reduction from higher-order to first-order. All HOI rules reduce (via SatisfyingSet) to FOI on the satisfying sets. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.6 ~p. 35–37; Ch. 10 §10.2 ~p. 203]

**C-2-20:** **Boolean AND rule (extensional, on predicates).** `(P AND Q).tv.s = P.tv.s · Q.tv.s` assuming independence. Heuristic — bad approximation when P,Q are dependent. [Source: Ch. 6 §6.2.4 ~p. 139; Ch. 10 §10.6.1 ~p. 209]

**C-2-21:** **Boolean OR rule.** `(P OR Q).tv.s = P.tv.s + Q.tv.s - P.tv.s·Q.tv.s`. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.6.1 ~p. 209]

**C-2-22:** **Boolean NOT rule.** `(NOT P).tv.s = 1 - P.tv.s`. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.6.1 ~p. 209]

**C-2-23:** **AND-elimination (forward).** `(P AND Q).tv.s, Q.tv.s ⊢ P.tv.s = (P AND Q).tv.s / Q.tv.s`. Similar for OR. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.6.1 ~p. 209]

**C-2-24:** **Boolean transformation equivalences (TV-preserving).** `P ⇒ Q ≡ ¬P ∨ Q`; `P ⇔ Q ≡ (¬P ∨ ¬Q)` (note: the book's rendering is `(¬P ∨ ¬Q)` but the second formula on p. 210 should be `(P ⇒ Q) ∧ (Q ⇒ P)` for equivalence — *see §5 contradiction flag*); `P ⇔ Q ≡ (P⇒Q)(Q⇒P)`. Executable in PLN without strength change. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.6.2 ~p. 210–211]

**C-2-25:** **Extensional term operators.**
- `A ANDExt B`: Member x (A ANDExt B) ⇔ Member x A ∧ Member x B
- `A MINUSExt B`: Member x A ∧ ¬ Member x B
- `A ORExt B`: Member x A ∨ Member x B
- Implication: `Subset x (A ANDExt B) ⇔ Subset x A ∧ Subset x B`
[Source: Ch. 10 §10.6.2.1 ~p. 211–213]

**C-2-26:** **Mixed-modal term operators.**
- `A ANDMix B` = (extensional intersection) ∧ (intensional union) — instances share ext of both; supersets include ints of both. Natural for "American Bank."
- `A ORMix B` = (extensional union) ∧ (intensional intersection).
[Source: Ch. 12 §12.5 ~p. 259–260]

**C-2-27:** **Intensional AND / OR / NOT.** `A ANDInt B` = intersection of pattern-sets; etc. Used for hypothetical cross-category creatures (e.g., `cat ANDInt dog`). [Source: Ch. 12 §12.5 ~p. 259]

**C-2-28:** **Variable Instantiation.** `F($X) <tv> ⊢ F(A) <tv1>` where `tv1` is computed via interpreting `s_1 = P(F($X) | Similarity $X A)` and decomposing as `I2M(deduction(M2I(F.tv), sim2inh(Similarity A $X.tv)))`. Correct only for cohesive satisfying-sets. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.7 ~p. 213–215]

**C-2-29:** **Implications Between Non-Variable-Bearing Relationships.** `Implication (Ben is ugly) (Ben eats beef)` is interpreted via possible-worlds: `Implication (Inh (Ben AND pu_C) ugly) (Inh (Ben AND pu_C) beef_eater)` and evaluated via variable instantiation. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.7.1 ~p. 216–217]

**C-2-30:** **Execution Output (ExOut) tautology.** `Execution $S $X (ExOut $S $X) <1,1>`. Conversion equivalence: `Evaluation F (ExOut A B) AND Execution A B $Y <1,1> ⇔ Evaluation F $Y`. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.8 ~p. 217–218]

**C-2-31:** **Combinatory Logic Rules.** Variables can be eliminated using B, C, I, S combinators: `Imp (very x) (I x) = ((B Imp very) x)(I x) = S(B Imp very) I x`. Cost: larger syntactic structures; benefit: simpler inference-control. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.9 ~p. 219–220]

**C-2-32:** **Unification Rule.** Given predicates `R, S`, find `T` with `SatSet(T) = SatSet(R) ∩ SatSet(S)` maximized. Fallback `T = R AND S` is correct only with independence; general algorithm is heuristic (e.g., pairwise matching for conjunctive compounds). [Source: Ch. 10 §10.10 ~p. 221–227]

### Family C — Quantifier Rules (Ch. 11)

**C-2-33:** **Direct Evaluation of ForAll.** Given indefinite TV for `F(t)`, derive TV for `ForAll x, F(x)` via third-order distribution: find fraction of first-order distributions "almost entirely" (threshold PCL) within the "essentially 1" interval `[1-e, 1]`. 7-step Monte Carlo procedure. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.2 ~p. 240–242]

**C-2-34:** **Direct Evaluation of ThereExists.** `ThereExists x, F(x) ≡ ¬ ForAll x, ¬F(x)`, inherits from ForAll via the ¬ transform. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.2 ~p. 242]

**C-2-35:** **Existential Generalization.** `F(c) <[L,U], b, k> ⊢ ∃x F(x) <[L,U], b, k>` (for c not involving x). Valid under indefinite TV. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.3 ~p. 242]

**C-2-36:** **Universal Specification.** `∀x F(x) <[L,U], b, k> ⊢ F(c) <[L,U], b, k>`. Valid via using the mean of `F(t)` across `t` as approximation. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.3 ~p. 242–243]

**C-2-37:** **Quantifier Exchange Rules.** `¬∀x F(x) ⇔ ∃x ¬F(x)`; `∀x ¬F(x) ⇔ ¬∃x F(x)`; `¬∀x ¬F(x) ⇔ ∃x F(x)`; `∀x ¬F(x) ⇔ ¬∃x ¬F(x)`. Hold under indefinite probabilities. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.3 ~p. 243]

**C-2-38:** **Constant Extraction from ∃ Rule FAILS in general.** `∃x: G ∧ F(x) ≠ G ∧ ∃x F(x)` under indefinite probabilities — holds only approximately when premise values are near 1. Flagged by the authors as a *violation* of classical predicate-logic equivalence. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.3 ~p. 243]

**C-2-39:** **Fuzzy Quantifiers (AlmostAll, AFew, Many).** Obtained by sliding the PCL range: `[LPC, UPC] = [0.9, 0.99]` represents AlmostAll; `[0.05, 0.1]` represents AFew. Parametrized sliding scale of all fuzzy quantifiers, all grounded in the same third-order probability machinery. [Source: Ch. 11 §11.4 ~p. 243–244]

### Family D — Intensional Rules (Ch. 12)

**C-2-40:** **Intensional Deduction.** No special rule: compute `P(A|B), P(C|B)` from IntensionalInheritance links plus term TVs, apply extensional deduction, then convert back. [Source: Ch. 12 §12.6 ~p. 260]

**C-2-41:** **Mixed Inheritance Deduction.** Decomposes into four cases: Ext-Ext, Int-Int, Ext-Int, Int-Ext; combine as weighted sum. Weights may be learned inferentially from analogous cases where ext/int values are known. [Source: Ch. 12 §12.6 ~p. 260–262]

**C-2-42:** **Intensional Similarity via Indefinite Probabilities.** Choose random property R of A with probability `f_R(A) = E(μ_A(R)) · woe(μ_A(R))`; estimate `E(μ_R(B))`; iterate; summarize as indefinite probability. Similar process with (R,Z) pair for IntensionalSimilarity. [Source: Ch. 12 §12.7 ~p. 263–264]

**C-2-43:** **TV Conversion Heuristics Inh↔Subset.** `Inheritance A B ⊢ Subset A B`: `s_Subset = (s_Inh - c)/(1 - c)`; reverse: `s_Inh = c + (1-c)·s_Subset`; `c` = system-wide average strength of IntensionalInheritance links. [Source: Ch. 12 §12.4 ~p. 258–259]

**C-2-44:** **Pattern-Intensity definition.** `IN(F,G) = [c(G) - c(F)]_+ · ASSOC(F,G)` where `[·]_+` is positive part, `ASSOC(F,G) = [P(F|G) - P(F|¬G)]_+`, and `c(·)` is complexity. Algorithmic-info-theoretic complexity can be *replicated* as a special case of probabilistic complexity via random program predicates. [Source: Ch. 12 §12.2 ~p. 250–253]

### Family E — Large-Scale Rules (Ch. 9)

**C-2-45:** **Multideduction.** Batch rule replacing iterated (deduction + revision):
`sAC = Σ_i (sAB_i · sB_iC + sA¬B_i · s¬B_iC)
     - Σ_{i≠j} [sB_iB_j · (...)·(wABiBj...) terms]
     - Σ_{i≠j} [s¬Bi¬Bj · (...)] + ε_1 + ε_2`
(Full formula with weight factors `w_ABiBj = d_BiA/(d_BiA + d_BjA)` given explicitly on p. 191.) Reduces compounding error from iterated deduction+revision. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.3 ~p. 186–192]

**C-2-46:** **Inclusion-Exclusion Approximation factor ε.** When computing `P(⋃ S_i)`, approximate using only first two terms of inclusion-exclusion with an additive "fudge factor" ε, pre-computed or empirically tuned per domain, combined with a `≤ 1` clamp. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.3.1 ~p. 188]

**C-2-47:** **Bayes-Net Augmented Inference.** A rule-base with shared premises is converted into a data table D (rows = `d · n` binary expansions per rule, columns = unique predicates + target), fed into standard Bayes-net structure learning; belief propagation then performs the inference. Used for static rule-bases in NCE. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.4 ~p. 195–196]

**C-2-48:** **Optimization-Based Inference (OBI).** Find atom-set minimizing `ε`-SP-inconsistency distance from initial atom-set, via stochastic local search. Distance metric: `d(V1,V2) = Σ c_{1i}·c_{2i}·|s_{1i} - s_{2i}|`. Avoids compounding error of iterated rule application. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.5 ~p. 196–199]

**C-2-49:** **Trail-Free Inference via Self-Organization.** OBI approximated by iterating: pattern-mine from environment → apply SP protocol → revise with new data. Iterated revision with fresh environmental input prevents circular-inference pathologies without explicit trails. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.5.1 ~p. 199–200]

### Family F — Temporal & Causal Rules (Ch. 14)

**C-2-50:** **Sequential AND (terms).** `SeqAND A B <s, T>` iff `AND(A, B) ∧ Initiation(B) - Initiation(A) ∈ T`. Means "B starts x seconds after A starts, x ∈ T." [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.1 ~p. 286–287]

**C-2-51:** **Disjoint SeqAND.** `DisjointSeqAND A B <s, T>` iff `AND(A,B) ∧ Initiation(B) - Termination(A) ∈ T`. "B starts x seconds after A finishes." [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.1 ~p. 287]

**C-2-52:** **Eventual SeqAND.** Same as SeqAND but without a specific `T`: `AND A B ∧ Evaluation after(SS_Initiation(B), SS_Initiation(A))`. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.1 ~p. 287–288]

**C-2-53:** **SeqAND (predicates, situation semantics).** `(SeqAND P Q)(S) <s,T>` iff `AND(P(S), Q(S)) ∧ timeof(Q,S) - timeof(P,S) intersects T`. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.1 ~p. 288]

**C-2-54:** **SimAND / SimOR.** `SimAND A B <s,T>` iff `HoldsThroughout(A,T) ∧ HoldsThroughout(B,T)`; existential variants use `HoldsSometimeIn`. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.1 ~p. 289]

**C-2-55:** **Predictive Implication.** `ExtensionalPredictiveImplication P Q <s,T>` ≡ `ExtensionalImplication P(S) (SeqAND(P,Q)<T>)(S)`. Intensional and mixed variants defined analogously. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.2 ~p. 289–290]

**C-2-56:** **Eventual Predictive Implication.** "If X continues long enough, Y occurs." Formally `Implication P(S) (EventualSeqAND(P,Q)<T>)(S)`. Example: `EventualPredictiveImplication starve die`. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.3 ~p. 290]

**C-2-57:** **Predictive Chain.** `PredictiveChain <s,(T1,...,Tn)> A1 ... An` ≡ `PredictiveImplication <s,Tn> (SeqAND<(T1,...,Tn-1)> A1...An-1) An`. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.4 ~p. 291]

**C-2-58:** **Predictive Deduction.** `PredImp A B, PredImp B C ⊢ PredImp A C` — must compute TV of `Imp A C` AND the odds that A precedes C. Time-interval is integrated-over; each (premise, time-point) pair gets full inference; results revised with time-distribution weights. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.5 ~p. 291–292]

**C-2-59:** **Causal Inference via Intensional PredImp.** Bogus causal relations signaled by high `ExtPredImp(A,B)` with low `IntPredImp(A,B)`. Real causality requires both. Rooster/dawn: high ext, low int because background knowledge (small forces can't move large objects) provides negative intensional evidence. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.5 ~p. 302–304]

**C-2-60:** **Causal Mechanism Simplicity Bias.** Weight PredImp count by simplicity of the causal-mechanism predicate: simpler mechanism → higher causal plausibility. Uses pattern-theory complexity. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.5.1.1 ~p. 304–305]

**C-2-61:** **Distal vs Local Cause / Enabling Conditions.** Handled via Novamente *importance* attention values (not pure PLN). Enabling conditions are PredImps with low importance (always-present preconditions). [Source: Ch. 14 §14.5.1.2 ~p. 305–306]

## §3. Inference Control Model

**C-3-1:** PLN supports two standard control modalities: **forward chaining** (from known atoms via matching input-filter to produce new atoms) and **backward chaining** (from target atoms via output-based-input-filter to produce required premises). [Source: Ch. 13 §13.2 ~p. 265–266]

**C-3-2:** The key difference from crisp logic theorem-proving: finding a single proof path is not enough — multiple paths yield multiple TV estimates that must be revised/chosen-between. The goal is *maximizing weight-of-evidence of target TV*, not just path finding. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.3 ~p. 266–267]

**C-3-3:** **Atom Structure Templates** define a predicate `A^(X)` true iff `X` matches Atom pattern A (possibly with variables). Used to describe which rules apply to which atoms. Can be combined via AND/OR and include TV-predicates (e.g., `HasTruthValueStrengthGreaterThan`). [Source: Ch. 13 §13.3.1 ~p. 267–268]

**C-3-4:** **Rule input-filter**: a vector of atom structure templates that accept arguments (used by forward chaining). **Output-based-input-filter**: computes which inputs are needed to produce a specific target (used by backward chaining). DeductionRule example: input filter is `[Inh $1 $2, Inh $2 $3]`; output-based-input-filter for target `Inh A C` is `[Inh A $1, Inh $1 C]`. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.3.2 ~p. 268–269]

**C-3-5:** **Backward-Chaining Algorithm (BIT).** Each step = expansion (use filters to find child nodes, insert into *expansion pool*) + evaluation (traverse BIT leaves-to-root, present children as Rule arguments, try all consistent variable-binding combinations). The BIT is treated evolutionarily: genotype = atoms+rules in the tree; fitness = weight-of-evidence at root. Fitness calculation uses Rule of Choice to combine multiple-path results. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.5 ~p. 270–271]

**C-3-6:** Variable-binding consistency checked at *evaluation stage*, not expansion stage — rationale: we don't know *a priori* which conflicting bindings are worse. Binding consistency means subresults' bindings don't conflict. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.5 ~p. 271]

**C-3-7:** **Retry Mechanism.** New search tree launched with pointer to partial tree each time a target atom's TV is re-attempted; implementation efficiency of retry is critical. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.5 ~p. 270]

**C-3-8:** **Simple BIT Pruning (Multi-Armed Bandit).** For each expansion possibility, assign utility `u`. For unexplored nodes: a priori based on rule usefulness and solution-space restrictiveness (prefer more-bound expressions, prefer direct lookup over hypothetical guess). For explored nodes: `u = d/E = weight_of_evidence / effort` or generalized `u = F^a / E^b` (fitness/effort powers). Selection via SoftMax: probability `∝ exp(u/T)` for temperature `T`. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.6 ~p. 271–272]

**C-3-9:** **Interdependent-Argument Pruning.** For Deduction-style rules where arguments share variables (e.g., Implication(A,$1) and Implication($1,C)): when one branch finds binding $1=B, clone parent BIT node with $1:=B and add to expansion pool. Resulting Implication(B,C) has higher a priori utility than Implication($1,C) (fewer free variables). Bandit utility modified to `u = (d_1 d_2)^a / E^b` accounting for pair-wise weight of evidence. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.7 ~p. 273–274]

**C-3-10:** **Forward-Chaining Utility.** Differs from backward-chaining by including an *interestingness* measure `I`: `u = I^c · d^a / E^b`. Interestingness definitions: (a) deviation from probabilistic independence; (b) difference between quick-effort and high-effort TV estimates; (c) how different target's actual TV is from TV if removed and re-inferred. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.8 ~p. 274–275]

**C-3-11:** **Shallow Forward Chaining as Concept Creation.** Select random atom combinations; run a few forward-chain steps; promising combinations persist in memory for future inference. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.8 ~p. 275]

**C-3-12:** **Inference Trails.** Non-quantitative objects attached to atoms, storing a partial list of atoms used to derive each TV. Updated at each inference step: conclusion's trail ← union of premises' trails + premises themselves. Used to (a) detect circular inference (non-empty trail intersection), (b) prevent false weight-of-evidence inflation from revising overlapping-evidence atoms, (c) explanation/debug. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.11 ~p. 125–127]

**C-3-13:** Trail lengths are *bounded* (e.g., 10 or 20) for memory reasons; hence, large-scale circular inference is *inevitable*. Wang's aphorism (quoted approvingly): "When circular inference takes place and the circle is large enough, it's called coherence." [Source: Ch. 5 §5.11 ~p. 127]

**C-3-14:** **Batch Reasoning Graph Inference (BRGI).** Alternative to per-step Rule of Choice for large static knowledge bases: construct a DAG where leaves are S-atoms, each non-leaf is derived from children via a rule. Quality measure: `c·N + (1-c)·T/N` where T = total expectation (strength deviation from 0.5 weighted by weight of evidence), N = inference count. Search via evolutionary programming or greedy graph-learning. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.2 ~p. 180–183]

**C-3-15:** **Rule of Choice.** Collection of heuristics for when revision is inappropriate — conflicting high-weight TVs, or trail-overlap circular-inference attempts. Four options: (a) distributional TV if conflict is from disjoint evidence, (b) Hypothetical-marked alternate versions, (c) expensive trail-merge inference, (d) default: use revision but set conclusion's weight = `max(d_1, d_2)` rather than sum. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.12 ~p. 127–129]

**C-3-16:** **Default Inference (penguin/bird).** NOT encoded as an explicit inference-rule — emerges from (a) deduction's independence assumption being wrong when conditioning on scope-violating attributes; (b) revision's treatment of wildly different strengths. Inference-control heuristic: arrange terms in a hierarchical DAG (e.g., maximizing strength·confidence within link-budget), then when reasoning about a term first use its immediate parents, ascending the hierarchy visiting each term only once. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.9 ~p. 275–277]

**C-3-17:** The Novamente Cognition Engine (NCE) ties PLN inference control together with other cognitive processes: evolutionary learning, stochastic pattern mining, adaptive attention allocation. PLN *alone* cannot solve combinatorial explosion for non-simple inferences — integration is explicitly required. [Source: Ch. 1 §1.5 ~p. 15–16; Ch. 13 §13.2 ~p. 266]

**C-3-18:** **Effort Management** is explicitly flagged as a *prominent* role for PLN control (more so than for crisp theorem proving): finding a first path is often easy; the question is how much compute to allocate to improving the conclusion TV via alternate paths. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.3 ~p. 267]

**C-3-19:** **Normalization** is not required in PLN (unlike some systems). As default, PLN does some light explicit normalization (EquivalenceLinks → paired ImplicationLinks) and implicit normalization (identifying an Atom A and a single-member ListLink of A). Holman's Elegant Normal Form is mentioned as a future experiment target. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.4 ~p. 269–270]

**C-3-20:** **URE / ECAN references: NOT PRESENT** in this 2008 edition of the book. The term "URE" does not appear. "ECAN" does not appear. Attention allocation ("importance" values with short/long-term horizons, NCE attention values) IS referenced as a Novamente-level mechanism outside PLN proper. [Source: Ch. 13 *passim*; Ch. 14 §14.5.1.2 ~p. 305–306 — gap inference]

**C-3-21:** **Pattern Schematization.** After backward-chaining produces a target atom, NCE's predicate schematization process produces "executable schemata" (action procedures). In the Fetch example this turned the proof tree into the schema `try goto Ball; try lift Ball; try goto Teacher; try drop Ball`. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.4.3 ~p. 294; §14.4.4 ~p. 296–297]

**C-3-22:** **Termination criterion.** Backward chaining continues until the target atom's truth-value weight exceeds a controller-set threshold (not on first path found). This makes effort management *necessary* rather than optional. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.2 ~p. 266; §13.3 ~p. 267]

## §4. Implementation-Relevant Claims

**C-4-1:** **Atom Type Hierarchy (implemented KR).** Terms (elementary and composite), Relationships (instances: Subset, Inheritance, IntensionalInheritance, Similarity, Implication, Equivalence, ExtensionalImplication, ExtensionalEquivalence, Member, Evaluation, Execution, ExOut, SatisfyingSet, Context, Hypothetical, AND, OR, NOT, ForAll, ThereExists, List, Set). Atoms have type labels, TV packages, and type-specific data. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.1, §2.4, §2.6 ~p. 23–37]

**C-4-2:** Atoms use specific notational shortcuts: `R A B <t>`, `R <t> \n A \n B` (nested), and object-field notation `(R A B).strength` or `.tv`. Variables prefixed with `$`. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.1 ~p. 25]

**C-4-3:** The `kick_curry` vs `kick_list` distinction: the same conceptual relation has two reified forms (list-argument and curried), related via `kick_List = listify kick_curry`. Both exist, stored with equivalence relations, for flexible inference. Conversion uses C-combinator: `kick_curry = C kick_curry_2` to swap argument order. [Source: Ch. 2 §2.7.2 ~p. 38–39]

**C-4-4:** **Hypothetical atoms** are markers. "Matt believes the Earth is flat" is encoded as `Evaluation believe Matt (Hyp (Inheritance Earth flat))` — the Hyp wrapper signals *inference inhibition*: atoms marked Hyp don't propagate TV into external inference. Hyp is NOT given "refined semantics" — just a marker + a small control-rule. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.11.1 ~p. 228–230]

**C-4-5:** **Higher-Order Statements vs Judgments (HOS vs HOJ).** Default is HOS (truth-values of embedded relationships are not part of the payload). HOJ is marked explicitly (e.g., `Implication_HOJ`) — required only for cases like "I am positive that countries rich in oil are moderately wealthy" where a specific embedded TV is semantically essential. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.11.2 ~p. 230–233]

**C-4-6:** **Context macro.** `Context C (Hyp R X Y)` ≡ `R (X ANDExt C) (Y ANDExt C)`. Simplifies common inferences and nudges the inference controller to combine context-matched premises. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.11.3 ~p. 233–234]

**C-4-7:** **BioLiterate Application.** PLN-HOI used as post-processor for biomedical NLP: parsed PubMed abstracts → logical relationships (via expert rules) → forward-chained PLN inference. Example result: "DLC prevents bone loss; cAMP prevents bone loss" — derived conjunction TV `<[0.0625, 0.358972], 0.9, 10>` (equivalent ≈ `<0.214, 6>`), judged to be more informative than a prior <1, 0.07> obtained via heuristic WoE formulas. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.12 ~p. 235–237]

**C-4-8:** **AGISim Fetch Learning.** Virtual agent learns fetch via backward-chaining from reward target. Uses perceptual pattern mining for SequentialAND/SimultaneousAND, primitive predicates `try(X)`, `can(X)`, `done(X)` with axiom `PredImp(SimAND(try X, can X), done X)`. Partial-reward structure is critical — final-reward-only doesn't work. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.4 ~p. 292–301]

**C-4-9:** Final learned schema is a SequentialAnd of 4 try-statements. The internal atom handles (e.g., `[9053948]`, `[6560272]`) are preserved in the inference trace for debugging. PLN performs "minimal but general-purpose" work. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.4.4 ~p. 297–301]

**C-4-10:** **Inference rule shorthands for Fetch:** `ModusPonensRule` (probabilistic Boolean MP, order-preserving for PredImps); `AndRules`; `SimpleANDRule` (independence-assumption conjunction); `RewritingRule` = composition of AndRule + ModusPonensRule; `CrispUnificationRule` (bookkeeping to bind variables in quantified expressions, preserving TV). [Source: Ch. 14 §14.4.3 ~p. 295–296]

**C-4-11:** **M2I/I2M interval-width multipliers.** Per-set cohesion parameter: more cohesive sets → smaller multiplier → less TV widening. Example given in §10.7: dead-terrorists more cohesive than dead-entities, multiplier 1.2 vs 1.5, yielding different conclusion intervals. Can be empirically computed if members known, or inferentially estimated from similar sets. [Source: Ch. 10 §10.7 ~p. 215–216]

**C-4-12:** **BIT Tree Construction Subtlety.** A whole new search tree must be created each time a target atom production is re-attempted; partial trees are preserved via pointers. The "retry mechanism" is a critical implementation-efficiency requirement. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.5 ~p. 270]

**C-4-13:** **Optimal Universe Size Formula is Pragmatic.** Setting `|U|` = sum of all atom counts (or multiple thereof), or context-restricted universe (e.g., Earth-relative for object reasoning). Formula: `n_U = n_B + (n_A - n_AB)(n_C - n_BC)/(n_AC - n_ABC)`. Test: ≈3% error on 650K-sentence corpus from few hundred triples. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.3 ~p. 87–89]

**C-4-14:** **Batch Reasoning Graph complexity note:** with `n` Bi nodes, multideduction's third-order term has `C(n,3)` cross-intersections; must be kept small in practice. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.3.2 ~p. 192]

**C-4-15:** **Bayes-Net Augmentation data-table format.** First column: target predicate P. Other columns: distinct left-hand-side predicates. Rows: for each rule I(k) with strength s_k discretized to s_dk (e.g., 11 levels), produce `d` rows — in `s_dk · d` of them first col = 1, rest = 0. Boolean combinations use doubled rows per disjunction (or random choice per disjunction for general Boolean formulas). Recommend normal-form (Holman's ENF) preprocessing. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.4 ~p. 195–196]

**C-4-16:** **OBI computational complexity.** Stochastic local search on large static atom-sets; or OBI on overlapping subsets with revision-based mediation. 1000-triangle experiment (3 inheritance relations each): mean strength 0.3; final-vs-initial 3-vector Euclidean distance ≈ 0.075. Deviation is manageable. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.5 ~p. 199]

**C-4-17:** **Indefinite TV inference is O(n1 · n2)** where `n1` = # second-order samples, `n2` = # first-order samples per second-order. Recommended `n1, n2` not specified; presumably domain-tuned. [Source: Ch. 6 §6.1 ~p. 131–133]

**C-4-18:** **Inverse n-from-[L,U]-b-k algorithm** is `O(log n)`: first double-search n until b-surplus detected, then binary-search between N and N/2. [Source: Ch. 4 §4.5.1.1 ~p. 61]

**C-4-19:** **Multideduction hand-computation shortcut**: `sA¬Bi = 1 - sABi`, `s¬BiC = (sC - sBi·sBiC)/(1 - sBi)`, `s¬Bi¬Bj = (1 + sBj - sBiBj·sBi)/(1 - sBi)`. These expansion formulas let you compute the ¬ terms from given strengths without extra stored data. [Source: Ch. 9 §9.3.2 ~p. 191]

**C-4-20:** **Implementation Emphasis.** PLN has been "implemented most of" in the NCE. PLN alone doesn't solve AGI; it must be combined with pattern mining, evolutionary learning, attention allocation. [Source: Ch. 1 §1.5 ~p. 15–16]

**C-4-21:** **Consistency Condition Enforcement.** Deduction/induction/abduction formulas multiplied by Heaviside products to zero-out invalid regions: e.g., `dedAC := [formula] · (H(sAB - max((sA+sB-1)/sA, 0)) - H(sAB - min(1, sB/sA))) · (H(sBC - max((sB+sC-1)/sB, 0)) - H(sBC - min(1, sC/sB)))`. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.4 ~p. 90–93]

**C-4-22:** **Time-distribution integration.** For PredImp inference with indefinite TVs: for each premise, integrate over time points weighted by time-distribution probability; for each (premise, time-point) combination, do a full inference; revise results with distribution-weighted revision. Adds a whole Monte Carlo level to the already-two-level inference. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.3.5 ~p. 291–292]

**C-4-23:** **Persistence / frame problem handling.** Event persistence is assumed *probabilistically* and *contextually* — not via circumscription (which the authors consider inadequate). Context identification is "plain old AI inference" — non-trivial but not temporally-special. [Source: Ch. 14 §14.2.2 ~p. 283–285]

**C-4-24:** **Appendix A NARS-comparison table** gives side-by-side TV formulas — useful for implementations bridging PLN↔NARS. Key: NARS and PLN (1D concept-geometry) diverge most strongly when `sA, sB, sC` are all large; converge when all small. [Source: Appendix A ~p. 307–319]

## §5. Open Questions, Gaps, and Internal Contradictions

**C-5-1:** **Authors admit PLN is not final.** "Our goal in articulating PLN has not been to present an ultimate and final approach to uncertain inference, but rather to present a workable approach... we expect the theory to evolve and improve." [Source: Ch. 1 §1.5 ~p. 16]

**C-5-2:** **Inference control combinatorial explosion unsolved.** "These heuristics do not solve the problem fully adequately, except for the case of fairly simple inferences." Explicit acknowledgment. [Source: Ch. 13 §13.2 ~p. 266]

**C-5-3:** **Revision-rule c-parameter requires dynamic tuning.** "So if c is to be set greater than zero, it must be dynamically adapted in such a way as to yield 'plausible' results, a notion which is highly domain-dependent." No general algorithm given. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.10.4 ~p. 120–121]

**C-5-4:** **Modus Ponens in PLN is "very bad."** "...this is a very bad estimate, it is clearly better than just setting P(poisonous) = .02 or P(poisonous) = .8*.7 which seem to be the only ready alternatives." Authors rate their MP rule as "highly error-prone heuristic." [Source: Ch. 5 §5.7 ~p. 110–111]

**C-5-5:** **M2I / I2M rules ill-defined for heterogeneous sets.** "They are badly wrong for Atoms representing random sets. They are also bad for sets like 'Things owned by Izabela' which are heterogeneous collections, not sharing so many common properties." [Source: Ch. 5 §5.8 ~p. 113]

**C-5-6:** **Independence-assumption-based deduction is provably biased.** For cohesive sets, the rule systematically *underestimates*: `E[f(x)] > sAB sBC + (1-sAB)(sC - sB sBC)/(1-sB)` (the formula). Authors introduce concept-geometry approach to mitigate, but "we have not yet derived an analysis of the error of these approximations" of the inverse-n approximation. [Source: Ch. 5 §5.6 ~p. 103; §4.5.1.2 ~p. 61]

**C-5-7:** **Constant-extraction-from-∃ is violated.** Classical predicate-logic equivalence `∃x: G ∧ F(x) ⇔ G ∧ ∃x F(x)` does NOT hold with indefinite TVs.
